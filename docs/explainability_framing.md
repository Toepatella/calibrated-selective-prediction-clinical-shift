## 1. Auditability as explanation (intro-ready)

When a clinician asks an AI system "why," two distinct questions hide inside the word. One is about the *world*: why does this scan indicate disease — which features, which biology, which causal pathway. The other is about the *model*: why should I trust this particular automated output, and when the system stays silent, why did it decline. This paper is squarely about the second question, and we argue that answering it faithfully is itself a form of clinical explainability — the form that is most actionable at the point of care and most defensible under distribution shift, where a confident-looking answer is exactly the thing a clinician most needs to be warned about. We call this stance **auditability as explanation**: an interpretable, per-case, subgroup-stratified account of the model's *own reliability* and of the reason behind every accept / defer / route decision, surfaced where the decision is made. This is the audit layer of the method (method_note §6): for each answered in-scope case it exposes the recalibrated posterior `σ̃(f(x))` (so the displayed confidence means what a clinician reads it to mean), a representativeness chip from the combined weight `ŵ(x,ŷ)` (how typical the case is of the data the model was calibrated and audited on), a cohort reliability flag `n_eff`, and a shift-regime badge; and for every declined case it names which reliability gate fired — uncertainty (`u(x) > τ̂`), suspected out-of-distribution (`o(x) > t̂_ood`), or untrustworthy covariate weight (`ŵ_cov(x) > w_max`) — together with the margin to each threshold (method_note §6.1–6.3).

The scholarly basis for treating this as explanation, rather than as mere instrumentation, is that an explanation is useful in a high-stakes setting to the precise extent that it is **faithful** — that it reflects what the model actually computes rather than a plausible-looking story about it (Jacovi & Goldberg, 2020; Lipton, 2018). Rudin (2019) makes the sharpest version of the point: a post-hoc explanation of a black box "must be wrong" in the sense that it cannot have perfect fidelity to the model, because "if the explanation was completely faithful to what the original model computes, the explanation would equal the original model, and one would not need the original model in the first place." The quantities our audit layer exposes are not post-hoc reconstructions of a black box's reasoning; they are the *same* finite-sample estimates and measured diagnostics the pipeline uses to make its accept/abstain/route decision (method_note §6). The displayed posterior is the recalibrated `σ̃` that enters the double-count corrector `Ẑ(x)`; the representativeness chip is the importance weight that reweights the target-risk estimate; the decline reason is the literal predicate that fired in the routing rule (method_note §5.1). Because the explanation *is* the decision machinery surfaced rather than approximated, it is faithful by construction in a way a saliency map over the input pixels cannot be (Adebayo et al., 2018). This is the form of transparency that recent work argues clinical ML most needs: a calibrated, communicated account of predictive uncertainty and of the model's own competence on the case (Bhatt et al., 2021), coupled to an abstain-with-reasons mechanism whose entire purpose is to improve trustworthiness by declining when the model should not be trusted (Hendrickx et al., 2024; Geifman & El-Yaniv, 2017).

This reframing maps directly onto the collection's explicitly stated targets — "explainability techniques designed for clinical auditability" and "Interpretable ML systems that ... support explainable decision pathways, improve AI literacy, and facilitate responsible deployment." It does **not** map onto the collection's other, equally explicit emphasis — explanations that help clinicians understand "complex patient dynamics," uncover "novel relationships," and enhance "causal reasoning" — and we are deliberate and honest about that boundary. Our system offers no account of disease mechanism, no feature-level biological narrative, and no causal claim about the patient; it explains the *model's* reliability and the reason it abstained or routed, not the medicine. We state this scope on purpose, because the most common failure of clinical XAI is to present a post-hoc rationalisation of a black box as if it were an account of the underlying biology. An honest explanation of *where and why a model should not be trusted* is, in a high-stakes setting, more useful and more defensible than an unfaithful story about why a patient is sick. Crucially, casting auditability as explanation introduces **no new guarantee**: every number the layer surfaces remains a finite-sample estimate (`x̂`) or a measured diagnostic under its own method's assumptions, and the separately-measured operating budgets `α_acc` and `α_ood` are not recombined into a deployed-pipeline certificate by the act of displaying them (method_note §6.5, §7.2).

> **Optional anchor.** A consensus clinical-AI guideline supports treating calibrated per-patient uncertainty, an OOD detector, and periodic auditing as named trustworthiness requirements rather than invented metrics. The FUTURE-AI guideline (Lekadir et al., 2025) is organised around six separate guiding principles — **F**airness, **U**niversality, **T**raceability, **U**sability, **R**obustness, and **E**xplainability — and the audit layer of this paper maps specifically onto two of them: **Traceability** (the per-case reliability panel, subgroup/fairness audit, and shift-regime logging) and **Explainability** (the per-case decline-attribution). We claim a mapping onto those two principles, not onto a single fused "Traceability + Explainability" target and not onto the other four. *(Reference verified live this session — see ledger.)*

## 2. Pre-empting "this is just UQ, not XAI"

A natural and forceful objection is that what we call explanation is merely uncertainty quantification (UQ) and trustworthy-ML plumbing wearing an explainability costume: the audit layer, the objection runs, never explains *why a prediction takes its value* nor anything about the patient — it reports reliability telemetry, and relabelling telemetry as "explanation" to fit the venue is a category error. We take the objection seriously and answer each prong on its own terms. The frame for the whole rebuttal is the **two-column trustworthiness-vs-explainability** split set out in `positioning_memo.md`: the bulk of the pipeline (recalibration, weighted risk control, OOD screening, the reliability telemetry) lives in the *trustworthiness* column and we make no explainability claim for it; the explainability claim is carried by **one separable artifact**, the routing-faithful DAR, which sits in the *explainability* column. Keeping the two columns distinct is what lets us concede that most of the layer is UQ while still defending a genuine, narrowly-scoped explanation. The DAR is **separable** in a strong sense: it can be lifted out, named, and evaluated (Section 3) on its own, and removing it would subtract the explanation without disturbing the trustworthiness machinery — so it is not "telemetry relabelled" but a distinct artifact that happens to be read off the same gate predicates.

**(a) "It gives no account of why the prediction is what it is."** We concede this outright: the panel does not explain why the classifier `f` assigns its label, and we do not claim it does. But the explanandum is different and legitimate. The object being explained is the **triage decision** — answer / defer / route — which is itself a consequential model action with a precise, recoverable cause. Explanation is contrastive and decision-relative: a good explanation answers "why this action and not the alternative" (Miller, 2019). The per-case decline-attribution is exactly that contrastive account — it names the gate that fired (the cause) and reports the margin to each threshold (how close the case came to being answered, or to being routed for a different reason). A post-hoc saliency map answers none of these for the routing decision, because it explains a classifier's pixel attention, not a gatekeeper's choice.

**(b) "Reliability is not explanation; a confidence interval is a quality-control statistic."** We agree that *some* of the layer's numbers are telemetry, and we say so. A cohort-level confidence interval on selective risk, taken alone, is a quality-control statistic, not an explanation. The explanation claim rests specifically on the **per-case decline-attribution**, and it meets a principled criterion that separates explanation from mere telemetry. A decision-explanation should be (i) **decision-linked** — it names the actual cause of *this* routing action, not a global property; (ii) **contrastive** — it gives the margin to the counterfactual at which the action would flip (Miller, 2019); and (iii) **contestable** — the clinician can check, challenge, or overturn it, which the contestable-AI literature argues is what makes an explanation actionable rather than performative: Ploug & Holm (2020) reconceptualise clinical explainability as *effective contestability*, holding that an explanation is adequate only insofar as it equips a stakeholder to contest the system's output rather than merely understand it. The decline-attribution (which gate, margin to each threshold) satisfies all three by construction; the bare interval does not. Conceding the interval is telemetry is what makes the rebuttal credible: we do not call every number an explanation, only the one that names a per-case decision cause. Independently, the interpretability literature does not treat UQ and explanation as disjoint — interpretability bundles distinct desiderata including trust and knowing when to rely on a model (Lipton, 2018), and communicating well-characterised predictive uncertainty is argued to be a *complementary form of transparency* to feature attribution (Bhatt et al., 2021).

**(c) "The call centres patient dynamics and causal reasoning, which are absent."** True, and stated plainly: our system does not explain disease processes or causal patient dynamics, and readers seeking that capability will not find it here. We do not paper over the gap or imply partial coverage of it. Instead we map precisely to the call's *other*, equally explicit language — its Trustworthy-ML bullet ("explainability techniques designed for clinical auditability") and its human-centered bullet ("explainable decision pathways ... facilitate responsible deployment"). Clinical auditability is a first-class solicited theme of the collection, not a backdoor; the contribution is on-scope for those clauses and explicitly off-scope for the patient-dynamics clause.

**(d) "The panel is statistician-facing, not a clinician explanation."** We separate two display tiers so this objection is answerable rather than merely denied. The **explanation surface** is clinician-facing: plain-language gate identity ("the model is too uncertain to answer," "this scan looks unlike the calibration data," "this case is outside the trusted weight region"), a margin rendered as "how close to being answered," and the resulting clinical action (act / defer to you / out of scope). The statistician-facing diagnostics — `σ̃` calibration, `n_eff`, `κ(Ĉ_S)` — are demoted to a reliability footer, not the explanation surface (method_note §6.4 gives the worked clinician read path). We do not assert clinician-friendliness; we point to the rendering and pre-register a clinician comprehension/usability evaluation as planned (and concede that until it is run, the clinician-facing claim is argued from design rather than measured).

**(e) "'Faithful by construction' is a slogan unless measured."** Inherent-interpretability claims (Rudin, 2019) are often asserted, not demonstrated, and a fair referee will demand the same faithfulness/stability scrutiny saliency receives (Adebayo et al., 2018; Arun et al., 2021). We therefore hold our own claim to that standard: we define faithfulness operationally (Jacovi & Goldberg, 2020) and pre-register pass/fail tests (Section 3 below) rather than asserting the property. The contribution is not a new guarantee dressed as explanation, but an explanation of a specific, real model action — the decision to answer, defer, or route — that is faithful because it is read directly off the mechanism that produces the action, and auditable because every reliability number beside it discloses its own assumption and measured degradation.

## 3. Routing-faithful decline-attribution (the named contribution)

### 3.1 Definition

We name the central explanatory artifact of the audit layer the **routing-faithful Decline-Attribution Record (DAR)**: a per-case explanation of *why the model declined to answer*, expressed as the set of reliability gates that fired together with the signed margin to each gate's threshold. Throughout, "routing-faithful" (faithful-by-construction) means **faithful to the routing decision, not to the diagnosis** — it asserts that the explanation matches the predicate the pipeline actually evaluated to route the case, *not* that the routing (or the underlying class prediction) is correct. We keep this scope clause attached to the term wherever it appears in the abstract and §6, so "faithful" is never read as "reliable" or "right." *(If a more clinical name is preferred, "Gate-Attributed Deferral" / "Decision-Pathway Attribution" reads well; pick one and use it consistently in the abstract and in method_note §6.)*

*Canonical source: the DAR is defined in [method_note.md](method_note.md) §6.6 (the method spine / single source of truth); the definition, firing set, and margins in §3.1–3.3 below must not diverge from it — this section adds the explainability framing and the saliency contrast, not a second definition.*

The deployed accept rule answers a case `x` only when all three gates pass — the case is confident enough (`u(x) ≤ τ̂`), in-distribution enough (`o(x) ≤ t̂_ood`), and inside the region where the covariate weight is trustworthy (`ŵ_cov(x) ≤ w_max`) — i.e. when the in-scope/answered event `A(x) = {g(x)=1} ∩ {o ≤ t̂_ood} ∩ {ŵ_cov ≤ w_max}` (with `g(x)=1[u(x)≤τ̂]`) holds (method_note §1.5.1, §5.1). When the case is routed or deferred instead, the DAR reports, for that `x`:

1. **The firing set** — which of the three predicates failed: uncertainty (`u > τ̂`, *defer to clinician*), far-OOD (`o > t̂_ood`, *route out of scope*), or untrustworthy covariate weight (`ŵ_cov > w_max`, *out of support*). These are **distinct clinical actions**, not one "rejected" bucket (method_note §5.1).
2. **The signed margin to each threshold** — `m_u = τ̂ − u(x)`, `m_ood = t̂_ood − o(x)`, `m_w = w_max − ŵ_cov(x)`. A negative margin marks a violated gate (a reason for the decline); the magnitudes show *by how much* the binding gate was crossed and *how close* the other conditions came to firing, so the clinician sees not only that a condition failed but how close the case sat to a different action.
3. **The measured operating context attached to the binding gate** — for an OOD decline, the measured far-OOD leakage rate of `t̂_ood` on the stated exposure set `O`; for an uncertainty decline, the cohort operating budget `α_acc` the threshold was tuned to; for a weight decline, the cohort `n_eff` and the clip-induced routing rate (method_note §4.3, §6.1, §6.3).

The DAR is the routing logic of method_note §5.1 turned outward: the same predicates the pipeline evaluates to make the decision are the predicates it reports.

### 3.2 Routing-faithful by construction (faithful to the routing, not the diagnosis), contrasted with post-hoc saliency

Faithfulness is the property that an explanation reflects the model's true decision process rather than a plausible substitute for it (Jacovi & Goldberg, 2020). Post-hoc feature attribution — saliency, Grad-CAM, SHAP, and relatives — cannot in general guarantee this: it builds a *second* artifact that approximates the model, and Rudin (2019) argues such an artifact "must be wrong" because "if the explanation was completely faithful to what the original model computes, the explanation would equal the original model." Worse, the approximation can be untethered from the model entirely: Adebayo et al. (2018) show that several widely used saliency methods are insensitive to randomising the model's parameters or the training labels, so the map a clinician sees may not depend on the model it claims to explain; and in medical imaging specifically, Arun et al. (2021) found that all eight saliency methods they tested on two chest-radiograph datasets (a pneumothorax-segmentation set and a pneumonia-detection set) failed at least one trustworthiness criterion (localization utility, sensitivity to model-weight randomization, repeatability across same-architecture retrains, and reproducibility across different architectures), warranting caution before clinical reliance. A saliency map over a chest radiograph offered as "the reason the system declined" would be exactly this kind of post-hoc story — visually plausible, of unverifiable fidelity, and explaining the *wrong object* (the classifier's attention, not the gatekeeper's choice).

The DAR has no such fidelity gap, because it is not an approximation of the decision — it *is* the decision predicate. The system declines a case if and only if some gate predicate in `A(x)` evaluated false (method_note §5.1); the DAR reports precisely the predicate(s) that evaluated false and the numerical margins the routing rule itself computed. There is no surrogate model, no learned attribution, and therefore no possibility that the explanation diverges from the computation: the explanation and the computation are the same conditional. This is the inherently-interpretable route Rudin (2019) advocates, applied not to the diagnostic classification (which remains a black box `f`) but to the **reliability gating** layered on top of it: the gate is a small, transparent, monotone rule over three named scores, and its output is its own faithful explanation. The contrast with saliency is along four axes — *explanandum* (decision rule vs. classifier pixel attention), *faithfulness* (identity vs. unverifiable approximation), *contrastiveness* (margin-to-threshold gives the counterfactual distance; a heatmap does not), and *stability* (the margin `m = threshold − score` is an exact affine function of the underlying score, so it inherits — and adds nothing to — that score's stability; any margin instability is the score's instability, which we measure (§3.4C/D) and which method_note §7.3 already flags as a component-method limit). Note that this last axis is **not** a stability guarantee for the explanation: it says only that the margin introduces no instability beyond the score's own, and the OOD/weight scores are themselves known to degrade under shift (method_note §4.4/§7.3). Note also that this is **not a head-to-head accuracy contest**: the DAR and saliency explain different objects, so the comparison demonstrates a faithfulness/stability *asymmetry*, not that one heatmap is "better-looking."

### 3.3 How it is surfaced in the per-case panel

The DAR sits in the clinician-facing surface of the audit layer (method_note §6.3–6.4). For a declined case the panel renders, in sequence: (i) the **plain-language firing reason** for the binding gate ("too uncertain to answer — deferred to you"; "looks unlike the calibration data — routed for review"; "outside the trusted weight region — routed"); (ii) a **distance-to-threshold** for the binding gate, rendered as "how close to being answered," with the non-binding margins available on demand so the clinician can see which constraint the case was next-closest to tripping; and (iii) the **resulting clinical action**. The measured operating context (§3.1 item 3) and the statistician-facing reliability diagnostics (`n_eff`, `κ(Ĉ_S)`, per-pair discriminator AUROC) are placed in a demoted reliability footer, not on the explanation surface (method_note §6.4, §6.5). For an *answered* case the panel instead shows `σ̃(f(x))`, the representativeness chip from `ŵ(x,ŷ)`, and the cohort `n_eff` flag, with `τ̂` shown as the reference line the case cleared.

### 3.4 Concrete, deadline-feasible faithfulness / stability evaluation

Faithfulness-by-construction is an architectural property, but we still evaluate it empirically — because a property can be designed in and yet implemented wrongly, and because *stability* is a separate desideratum that faithfulness does not imply. All checks below are pre-registered (consistent with `preregistration.md` §5), measured on held-out target data, reported with sampling uncertainty, and stratified by the pre-registered subgroups (preregistration §5.3, §6.3). They are cheap: (A)–(B) are executable invariants over already-computed gate scores, requiring no new model training; (C)–(D) reuse the existing augmentation family and calibration folds; only (F) needs human subjects and is marked planned/IRB-gated.

**Deliverable triage (what ships even on the tightest timeline).** The **CORE, deadline-feasible explainability deliverable** is check **(A)** — the construction-faithfulness invariant — **plus a minimal but runnable panel renderer** that reads cached gate scores and logits and emits the per-case DAR surface of §3.3 (firing reason, distance-to-threshold, resulting action) and the answered-case cues of §3.4(G). Together these demonstrate the named contribution end-to-end on real cached outputs: the invariant proves the artifact is faithful-by-construction, and the renderer proves it actually renders. Checks **(C)–(E)** (perturbation stability, re-estimation stability, saliency-baseline contrast) are **insurance** — they strengthen the faithfulness/stability story and are cut first if we fall behind, in roughly that priority order. Check **(F)** (clinician comprehension) stays **IRB-gated / planned** regardless of timeline. This triage is a planning commitment, not a claim: it states which artifacts will exist by the deadline, not what they will show.

- **(A) Construction-faithfulness — an executable invariant.** For every declined case, assert in code that the DAR's reported firing set equals the set of gate predicates the routing rule actually evaluated false, and that each reported margin equals the recomputed score-minus-threshold. Report the discrepancy rate; the target is exactly **zero**. This operationalizes "faithful by construction" as a machine-checkable invariant rather than a slogan — a non-zero rate is a *bug*, not a modelling residual. No analogous exact check exists for the saliency baseline.
- **(B) Counterfactual / margin validity (necessity & sufficiency).** Because the DAR names the violated gate(s), it makes a falsifiable counterfactual claim — but the exact form of that claim depends on the firing-set cardinality, and we state it conditionally to avoid overclaiming. For a **single-gate decline** (exactly one violated gate, all others passing with positive margin), the claim is exact: nudging the binding score exactly to its threshold flips the decision to *answer* (sufficiency), and nudging a non-binding score within its passing region does *not* change the decision (necessity of the named gate). For a **multi-gate decline** (two or more violated gates), moving a single binding score to its threshold leaves the case routed by the others, so the by-construction flip is defined only for moving **all** violated scores to their thresholds simultaneously. We therefore report the flip-rate **conditioned on firing-set cardinality**: (i) single-gate declines — flip-rate when the lone binding score is moved to the threshold (target: high, by construction) and decision-invariance under non-binding perturbations (target: high); (ii) multi-gate declines — flip-rate when all violated scores are moved to their thresholds together (target: high, by construction), with single-score moves reported as *not* expected to flip. The rate and composition of multi-gate declines is the informative number, which the DAR reports as such. This is the model-sensitivity logic of the saliency sanity checks (Adebayo et al., 2018) applied to the decline explanation.
- **(C) Stability under input perturbation.** Apply clinically plausible, label-preserving perturbations (small rotations/translations, intensity/stain jitter, mild noise/compression within a pre-registered acquisition-realistic family) and report (i) the binding-gate flip rate and (ii) the distribution of `|Δ margin|` for each of `m_u`, `m_ood`, `m_w`, **stratified by distance-to-threshold**. A faithful, stable decision pathway should show small margin drift and rare binding-gate flips except near a threshold; reporting flip-rate vs. distance-to-threshold attributes instability to genuinely borderline cases rather than to explanation noise. We do not claim stability is a guarantee — we measure it and report where it is low (method_note §6.5).
- **(D) Stability under re-estimation / retraining.** Re-estimate the thresholds across calibration folds, and retrain `f` (or vary seeds), then report agreement of the binding-gate label and rank-correlation of margins for matched cases. This is the reproducibility analogue of the Arun et al. (2021) cross-retraining criterion.
- **(E) Baseline contrast — post-hoc saliency under the same sanity checks.** For the same declined cases, generate a post-hoc saliency explanation over the input (e.g. an attribution map for the binding score or the classifier logit) and subject it to the *same* model-parameter-randomization and label-randomization sanity checks (Adebayo et al., 2018) and the Arun et al. (2021) repeatability/reproducibility metrics. The pre-registered expectation, grounded in that work, is that the saliency baseline may be insensitive to randomization (the documented failure mode), whereas the DAR's check (A) is exact and check (B) is sensitive by design. Frame the comparison honestly as a faithfulness/stability asymmetry, not an accuracy race. Concrete baselines: a gradient-based saliency map (Grad-CAM; Selvaraju et al., 2017) and/or a feature-attribution baseline (SHAP; Lundberg & Lee, 2017).
- **(F) Clinician comprehension (planned / IRB-gated).** A small reader study testing whether clinicians correctly infer the firing gate and the appropriate action from the rendered DAR. If not run for this paper, state it as planned and concede the clinician-facing claim is otherwise argued from design (method_note §6.4), not yet measured. The actionability criterion the study would test — that a clinician can read the DAR, infer the firing gate, and take the right contestation/override action — is the *effective contestability* requirement of Ploug & Holm (2020).
- **(G) Answered-case co-variation — the accept-side counterpart.** Checks (A)–(F) all interrogate the *declined* side; the accept side needs its own measurement so the panel's answered-case cues are audited too. Mirroring preregistration §5.5, bin the **answered** in-scope cases by the panel cues a clinician actually reads — the representativeness chip from `ŵ(x,ŷ)`, the `n_eff` cohort-reliability regime, and the shift-regime badge — and report the **measured accepted error per bin** with sampling-uncertainty intervals (and stratified by the pre-registered subgroups). This checks that the displayed reliability cues *co-vary* with realized accepted error in the expected direction — that cases shown a "less representative" chip or a low-`n_eff` flag carry measurably higher accepted error than cases shown reassuring cues — so an answered-case panel is informative rather than decorative. It is a measurement only: it reports whether the cues track error, not a guarantee that they do, and a flat or inverted profile is a finding to disclose, not a gate. Like (A)–(E) it is **no-IRB and deadline-feasible** — it reuses the cached gate scores, weights, and held-out target labels already computed for the coverage decomposition, requiring no new model training.

### 3.5 Explicit limit (no overreach, no guarantee smuggling)

The DAR faithfully explains **the routing decision** — *which reliability condition failed and by how much* — and nothing more. It does **not** explain why the classifier assigned its label, and it does **not** explain the disease, the biology, or the causal dynamics of the patient. It also does not explain *why a gate's underlying score took its value* (e.g. why `o(x)` was high), only that it did and that this is why the case was routed. The thresholds `τ̂`, `t̂_ood`, `w_max` are **tuned operating points, not certified bounds**, so the margins are signed distances in score space, **not** calibrated probabilities of error and **not** a per-case guarantee; `α_acc` and `α_ood` remain separately-measured operating budgets, never a certified split (method_note §5.3, §6.5). Finally, the scores the DAR thresholds inherit the component-method limits of method_note §7: the OOD score carries no distribution-free guarantee (Fang et al., 2022, as cited in method_note §4.4/§7.1), and the covariate weight is *estimated*, not oracle. So a faithful attribution to the OOD or weight gate is a faithful report of a *heuristic screen's decision*, not a certificate that the case is truly OOD or truly unrepresentative. The DAR makes the model's routing reasoning auditable; it does not make the underlying scores infallible, and the panel says so.

Finally, the **interface itself has documented failure modes that our evaluation does not rule out**. Surfacing a per-case reliability panel can induce **automation bias** — a clinician over-trusting a reassuring panel and under-scrutinising an answered case — and the recurring decline reasons and reliability flags can drive **alarm fatigue**, where frequent or repetitive warnings are progressively ignored. Checks (A)–(E) measure the panel's *fidelity* (that it faithfully reports the routing decision and that its cues track measured error, per §3.4(G)); none of them measures the panel's *effect on clinician decisions*. The interface is therefore **fidelity-measured but not decision-validated**: only the planned/IRB-gated reader study (§3.4(F)) begins to probe behavioural effects, and even it does not establish improved patient outcomes. We claim a faithful, auditable explanation surface, not a validated decision aid, and we flag these interaction risks as open and unmeasured.
